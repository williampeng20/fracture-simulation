In this Project, we will be implementing a system for object collisions and simulating the fractures from these collisions. We will also be using this system to simulate different types of materials and how realistically they will interact with each other.

Problem Description

Watching any modern Hollywood movie, the common destructive effects such as explosions and large-scale collisions become impossible for practical effects to be used. Therefore, the industry has turned to using computer graphics to create these effects. Simulating the collision, fracturing, and destruction of objects will allow us to solve this problem, but the challenge lies in detecting a collision between objects, and then calculating how a collision changes the topology of the objects, even creating multiple sub-objects. This is important as in the real world, when objects collide there is the possibility that one of the objects takes too much force and breaks from impact. This system will allow us to simulate this interaction and model it in the digital space. Building this system will be challenging, as we will need to determine the way and place pressure is accumulated in different materials to model realistic fracturing behavior.

Visuals and Deliverables
What we plan to deliver

We will divide the objects we are presenting into two categories: Imapct objects and stressors. Our visuals will clearly demonstrate different crucial stages in our impact and fracturing simulation. The impact object structure will be a wall and will differ between its materials; this includes wood, glass, clay and concrete. The stressors will be a basebal, a bullet and a bat - all with appropriately realistic material makeups. We will demonstrate accelerated impacts (such as a bullet being fired) as well as the effects of the stressors falling on the impact objects. We will define our crucial impact stages into three categories: pre-impact, mid-impact and post-impact with most focus on mid-impact and using post-impact and pre-impact mostly as visual frames of reference. Each of these scenes will be focused on the two objects that will impact, meaning that lighting will only be used to make our objects of interest clear and visible and our backgrounds will be kept minimal so as to not be distracting.

How to measure the quality/performance of our system

The performance of our system will be defined by the amount of particles we can represent in our simulation, the higher being better as it can represent simulations in higher definition. Overall, our performance will scale to the robustness of our system. Some elements that would define this would include the complexity of the mesh we are able to represent, in terms of both number of triangles, as well as the number of different materials within a mesh. The ability to toggle variables such as projectile weight, speed, and acceleration: while a stretch goal, would make our system extremely robust in its ability to represent a greater range of simulations. The accuracy of our system will be defined by it’s adherence to the laws of physics. Both the effects of gravity and the destruction of the material must realistically model the effects of the forces arising from the projectile colliding with the brittle material. Potentially we can replicate a few scenarios in real life to compare if the brittle material behaves in the correct fashion, but this process may be irreproducible and cost intensive. It can provide a rough estimate of fracture geometry which we can use to error-check our own results.

What questions do we hope to answer with our analysis

Through our analysis, we hope to answer whether a realistic modeling of brittle material fracturing can be achieved. If this is the case, we would hope to understand whether the interaction between a projectile and a brittle material colliding is a deterministic one. Additionally, if our system is built up to include features such as multi-material meshes, or user modifiable meshes, we would hope to answer more questions on mesh structural strength. Which structural features of a mesh of certain properties can help reduce fracture impact? What makes a mesh strong and how can a mesh be reinforced with minimal complexity? Conversely, what structural features are "weak spots" in a mesh?
